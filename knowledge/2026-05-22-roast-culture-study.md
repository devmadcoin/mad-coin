## The Punch Up Rule

**Core principle:** Comedy aimed at those MORE powerful is empowering and relatable. Comedy aimed at those LESS powerful is oppression.

- Punching UP: Mocking politicians, rich families, authoritative groups, systemic structures
- Punching DOWN: Mocking marginalized groups, victims, the powerless

**$MAD Application:**
- ✅ Roast tradfi, BlackRock, the 401k system, "the economy" — these are the powerful
- ✅ Mock the fear-mongering media that keeps people small
- ❌ Never mock individual holders, small accounts, or people who paper-handed
- The target must be a SYSTEM or INSTITUTION, never a person

## Roast Comedy Format (Friars Club → Dean Martin → Modern)

**Why roasts get away with more:**

2. **Playful transgression** — it's clearly a performance, not genuine hostility

## Comedy Writing Mechanics

### The Rule of Three
- Three setups build tension → fourth is the punchline
- Example: "If you want to be seen — stand up. If you want to be heard — speak up. If you want to be appreciated — shut up."
- The first two create pattern expectation → third breaks it

**$MAD Application:**
- "Your 401k is BlackRock's asset. Your mortgage is the bank's asset. Your time is your boss's asset. GET $MAD."
- Three truths → one solution

### Punch the Last Word
- Let the voice RISE on the last word or two of the punchline
- It's the final words that give the joke life
- The setup is flat → punchline rises

**$MAD Application:**
- "You were born to be MORE than a quarterly report." → rise on "report"
- "The world is only going to GET MORE $MAD." → rise on "$MAD"

## What Makes Roasts Land vs. Backfire

### Lands:
1. **Specificity** — General insults miss. Specific observations hit.
2. **Shared truth** — The audience must recognize the target
3. **Timing** — The pause before the punch is as important as the punch
4. **Confidence** — Hesitant delivery kills even good material

### Backfires:
1. **Punching down** — Targets the powerless
2. **Forced edginess** — Controversy for its own sake
3. **No point of view** — Random insults without a thesis
4. **Over-explaining** — If you have to explain why it's funny, it's not

## Social Media Comedy Specifics

### The Scroll Stop
- Under 280 chars for the hook
- Visual element multiplies impact 10x
- The first 3 words determine if they keep reading

### The Share Trigger
People share comedy that:
- Makes them look smart ("I get this reference")
- Expresses what they already feel but couldn't articulate
- Has a clear target everyone hates
- Is quotable and meme-able

**$MAD Voice Checklist:**
- [ ] Is the target a system, not a person?
- [ ] Is there a clear thesis (not just random insults)?
- [ ] Is it under 280 chars for the hook?
- [ ] Does it express what the community already feels?
- [ ] Is the punchline the LAST thing they read?
- [ ] Would someone quote this with "— $Mad Claw"?

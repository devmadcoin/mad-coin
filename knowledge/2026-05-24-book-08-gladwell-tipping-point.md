# Book Study: The Tipping Point — Malcolm Gladwell (2000)

## The Core Idea

**"Ideas and products and messages and behaviors spread like viruses do."**

Gladwell's thesis: Social epidemics don't happen gradually. They reach a **critical mass** — a "tipping point" — where momentum shifts dramatically and the idea goes from invisible to ubiquitous almost overnight.

Small changes, the right people, the right message, and the right context — these are the levers that tip an epidemic.

## The Three Rules of Epidemics

### 1. The Law of the Few
A small number of people are responsible for the majority of spread. Three types:

**Connectors** — People with vast social networks who bridge different worlds.
- They know everyone. They're the social glue.
- They spread information widely because their network spans multiple clusters.
- Example: Paul Revere's midnight ride worked because he was a Connector.

**Mavens** — Information specialists who accumulate knowledge and share it.
- They LOVE to share what they know.
- They start word-of-mouth epidemics because their recommendations are trusted.
- Example: The person everyone asks for restaurant recommendations.

**Salesmen** — Persuaders with charisma who convince others to act.
- They have an intangible ability to influence through personality.
- They're the ones who close the deal.

**The insight**: You don't need millions of people to start an epidemic. You need a few of the right people.

### 2. The Stickiness Factor
The content of the message matters. It must be **memorable and actionable**.
- "Sesame Street" vs. "Blue's Clues" — both educational, but Blue's Clues was stickier because it engaged the viewer actively.
- Small changes to how a message is packaged can make it dramatically more sticky.
- The message must "reside in the host long enough to take."

### 3. The Power of Context
Human behavior is sensitive to and strongly influenced by its environment.
- **Broken Windows Theory**: Small signs of disorder lead to more serious crime.
- The "context" of a community shapes what behaviors are acceptable.
- Small environmental changes can tip large behavioral shifts.
- Example: NYC crime drop in the 1990s — cleaning graffiti and fixing broken windows changed the context.

## The 150 Rule (Dunbar's Number)

Groups tip when they exceed ~150 members.
- Below 150: Social pressure works. Everyone knows everyone.
- Above 150: Social pressure breaks down. You need hierarchy, rules, structure.
- This is why successful communities, cults, and companies often cap groups at ~150.
